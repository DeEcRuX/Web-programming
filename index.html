Nepali New Year/
Bisket Jatra

Nepali Calender : वैशाख

Solar Calender : April-May

Active Region : Bhaktapur,Nationwide

The Nepali New Year, also known as "Bikram Sambat," is a time of renewal and reflection. As the old year comes to a close and a new one begins, it is an opportunity to let go of the past and embrace the possibilities of the future. The new year symbolizes a fresh start and the chance to live life with a renewed sense of purpose. It also serves as a reminder to be grateful for the blessings of the past year and to strive for growth and self-improvement in the year to come. On this day, children (and many adults too!) offer sweets, fruits and gifts to their mothers to show their respect. Those whose mothers have passed away visit Mata Tirtha in the west of Kathmandu, take a holy bath and make offerings in their mother’s memory.

The New Year is always a lively time to be in Nepal, with mass celebrations taking place all over the country. A particularly lively place to spend the day is Bhaktapur, where the Bisket Jatra festival takes place. A huge chariot carrying the god Bhairab is pulled through the streets, ending with a chariot battle at Bhaktapur’s Khalna Tole.

Buddha Jayanti

Nepali Calender : वैशाख / ज्येष्ठ

Solar Calender : May-June

Active Region : Swayambhunath, Bodhnath, Lumbini, Nationwide

The Lord Buddha’s birthday falls on the first full day of the first month of the Hindu lunar calendar, and is celebrated by Hindus and Buddhists alike. It is observed at Buddhist shrines and monasteries throughout Nepal, but a particularly grand ceremony is held at Buddha’s birthplace in Lumbini. In Kathmandu, the Tibetan enclave of Boudhanath is a particularly good place to watch the festivities.

The Buddha Jayanti, also known as Vesak or Buddha Purnima, is the celebration of the birth, enlightenment, and passing away of Lord Buddha. The philosophy behind Buddha Jayanti is to strive for inner peace, wisdom and compassion through the path of non-violence and self-control. It is an opportunity to contemplate on the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path, which are the foundations of Buddhism, and to work towards achieving the ultimate goal of Nirvana, or enlightenment. It is a time to let go of our attachments and to focus on the present moment, to cultivate a peaceful and content mind. Celebrating Buddha Jayanti is not just an annual event, but a reminder to live a meaningful life, by following the path of the Buddha.

Rato Machhindranath Jatra

Machh

Nepali Calender : वैशाख / ज्येष्ठ

Solar Calender : April-May

Active Region : Patan

This is Nepal’s longest and largest festival, held in Patan (or Lalitpur). A giant chariot is built on Pulchowk Road over several weeks, before an effigy of the god Machchhendranath is placed inside. Three days later, the chariot begins its procession all throughout Patan and wider Lalitpur, towards Bungamati.

It is a celebration of Newari god of rain, Machhindranath, and is held annually to pray for the monsoon rains to come in time and bring prosperity to the land. The festival is a reminder of the delicate balance that exists between humanity and nature. It highlights the importance of respecting and honoring the forces of nature that sustain us.The festival encourages us to reflect on our relationship with the environment. It also serves as a reminder of the impermanence of life and the need to live in the present moment.

Naag Panchami

Nepali Calender : श्रावण

Solar Calender : July - August

Active Region : Nag Pokhari in Naxal, Taudaha, Nationwide

Naag Panchami falls right in the middle of the monsoon.Naag Panchami is usually celebrated on the fifth day of the bright half of the lunar month of Shravana, which is considered to be an auspicious month in Hinduism. This Hindu festival worships the serpent god, Naag, and pictures of Naag are posted in doorways, as well as offerings of milk. It is believed that worshipping Naag protects against snake bites. This festival marks respect to serpents as water guardians, and to ensure regular rainfall in the Kathmandu Valley.

Philosophically, the serpent symbolizes the power of energy that lies dormant within us all, waiting to be awakened. It represents the kundalini energy, which when awakened, can lead to spiritual enlightenment. The festival of Naag Panchami serves as a reminder that within each of us is the potential for spiritual growth and transcendence, and it is up to us to tap into that power and awaken our true selves.

Janai Purnima

Nepali Calender : भाद्र

Solar Calender : August - September

Active Region : Hilly Region,Nationwide

Janai Purnima is the Sacred Thread Festival, when Hindu men, especially the Brahmins and Chettris, perform their annual change of Janai. Everyone who celebrates this festival puts a sacred thread around their wrist. Gosaikunda, a sacred pond at high altitude, is the hub of this festival and sees a hive of colour and activity on this day.

Janai Purnima is a Hindu festival that celebrates the sacred thread worn by men, known as the janai, and symbolizes the bond between the individual self and the universal self. It is a day for self-reflection and the reaffirmation of one's commitment to living a virtuous and meaningful life. The thread, worn around the wrist, serves as a reminder to always strive towards spiritual growth and to live in harmony with the world around us. On Janai Purnima, one may also perform purification rituals and seek blessings from spiritual leaders and elders. In a philosophical way, Janai Purnima is a time to reconnect with the inner self and to transcend the ego, leading to a state of unity and oneness with the divine.

Gai Jatra

Nepali Calender : भाद्र

Solar Calender : August - September

Active Region : Kathmandu

Gai Jatra (literally meaning the festival of cows) is a fusion of three traditions that came into being in three different periods of time. The first and the oldest tradition incorporates a cult and worship of the ancient god of death – Yamaraj. Despite the deathly theme, it is actually as time full of music, dancing and laughter. The festival marks the acceptance and celebration of death in a positive way, as an inevitable part of life. Every family who has lost a member, in the previous year, is supposed to lead an intricately decorated cow through the city. In the absence of a cow, a boy dressed as a cow can take on the role.

It is a celebration of life and death, and a reminder of the fleeting nature of existence. The festival is a time to remember and honor the deceased, and to come to terms with the reality of death and loss. It also serves as a reminder to live fully and make the most of the time we have, for it is uncertain when our time will come. The cows that are paraded during the festival symbolize the journey of the soul, and the belief that all living beings will eventually return to the earth. Gai Jatra is a profound and meaningful tradition that encourages reflection on the deeper aspects of life, death and the human condition.

Krishna Janmastami

Nepali Calender : भाद्र

Solar Calender : August - September

Active Region:Terai Region, Kathmandu, Nationwide

Krishna Janmashtami is a celebration to mark the birth of Krishna. Considered the eighth incarnation of Lord Vishnu, Krishna is the most important character in the Hindu epic – the Mahabharat. During this festival, Hindu devotees visit Krishna temples. In particular, thousands of devotees gather at the stone Krishna Mandir in Patan Durbar Square.

It is believed that Lord Krishna is the embodiment of love, compassion and wisdom, and his teachings continue to inspire and guide many people to this day. Philosophically, the celebration of Janmashtami serves as a reminder to strive for self-improvement and to seek inner peace, enlightenment and the ultimate realization of one's true self. The festival also encourages to reflect on the idea of the divine and its manifestation in the world around us, and to embrace the spiritual path of devotion and surrender to the ultimate reality.

Haritalika Teej

Nepali Calender : भाद्र

Solar Calender : August - September

Active Region : Nationwide

Teej is a festival celebrated exclusively by Nepali women, with festivities often stretching over 3 days around the month of August. Dressed in red saris and a red tika, the women sing and dance for the long lives of their husbands, and a long and firm relationship until death parts them. Teej is observed for marital happiness, the well-being of spouse and children, and the purification of own body and soul. Teej is the most famous festival among Nepali women.

Teej is a festival that celebrates the onset of monsoon and the reunion of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati. But it goes beyond just the celebration of a natural event, it's also an opportunity to reflect on the cyclical nature of life. It reminds us that just as the rains bring new life to the earth, so too can we strive to rejuvenate ourselves. The festival encourages us to let go of the past and embrace the present, to let go of the negative and welcome the positive, and to celebrate the beauty of nature and the sanctity of relationships. Teej reminds us that, like the monsoon, life is a cycle of birth, growth, decay, and rebirth, and that by embracing and celebrating these changes, we can find true joy and fulfillment.

Indra Jatra

Nepali Calender : भाद्र

Solar Calender : August - September

Active Region : Kathmandu

Indra Jatra is a colourful street festival held each year in Kathmandu, in honour of the Hindu god Indra. The celebrations consist of two events; Indra Jatra with its masked dances of deities and demons, and Kumari Jatra which is a chariot procession for the living goddess Kumari. Centred around Kathmandu Durbar Square, the celebrations last for eight days. This is also a time for family members who have died during the past year to be remembered

Indra Jatra, also known as Yenya, is a festival celebrated in Nepal and by the Newar community in Nepal. The festival is celebrated in honor of Lord Indra, the god of rain and fertility, and is intended to ensure a bountiful harvest. On a deeper philosophical level, however, the celebration of Indra Jatra can be seen as a reminder of the cyclical nature of life and the importance of understanding the interconnectedness of all things. The festival serves as a reminder that, just as the rains are necessary for the growth of crops and the sustenance of life, so too are the struggles and hardships that we face necessary for our growth and development as individuals. It is also a reminder that everything is interconnected and that everything we do has an impact on the world around us.

Bijaya Dashami

Nepali Calender : आश्विन - कार्तिक

Solar Calender : September - October

Active Region : Nationwide

Dashain is the most important festival to Nepalis, and stretches over 2 weeks with various stages and events. It is a celebration of good prevailing over evil, and most families offer male goats, ducks, chickens, eggs and coconuts to the goddess Durga. Many Nepali people return to their home villages at this time to spend the fifteen-day festival with their families. Large swings are set up for children, and from the tenth day, family members receive Tika (rice, red vermillion and yoghurt) on their foreheads from their elders.

Bijaya Dashami is a festival that symbolizes the victory of good over evil, and the triumph of knowledge over ignorance. This festival has a deep philosophical significance as it reminds us that in life, we will face many obstacles and challenges. But with determination, perseverance and knowledge, we can overcome them and be victorious. The festival is also a reminder that the path to victory is not easy, it requires continuous effort and the willingness to learn and grow. The festival also teaches us that the ultimate victory is not just about external achievements but also about inner growth and self-realization. Therefore, Bijaya Dashami is a call to reflect on our own inner battles and to strive for victory over our own limitations.

Tihar

Nepali Calender : कार्तिक

Solar Calender : October - November

Active Region : Nationwide

Tihar is Nepal’s second most important festival, after Dashain. In each of the three days, a different deity is worshipped: on the first day the crow, the messenger of Yama and the bringer of death; on the second, dogs, which are believed to be Yama’s custodian; and on the third, the goddess Lakshmi is worshipped, the bringer of wealth. Lakshmi is worshipped by lighting houses with oil lamps, candles and colourful lights.

Tihar, also known as Deepawali, is a festival of lights celebrated in Nepal and by Nepali communities around the world. It is a time of joy, reflection, and gratitude. The festival is a celebration of the triumph of good over evil, and the victory of light over darkness. It is a reminder that even in the darkest of times, hope and positivity can prevail. The festival is also an opportunity to reflect on one's actions and to make amends for any mistakes or wrongs that were committed during the previous year. Through the celebration of Tihar, we are reminded that the choices we make, and the actions we take, have a profound impact on the world around us. It is a time to be mindful of our actions and to strive for compassion, kindness, and understanding in our interactions with others.

Chhath

Nepali Calender : कार्तिक

Solar Calender : October - November

Active Region : Terai Region

Chhath is the most important festival observed in the Terai region and falls on the seventh day after Tihar. Devotees fast and make offerings to the sun by gathering at river banks. The festival is dedicated to the Hindu solar deity, Surya, and the goddess Shashthi. The Terai region is the best place to observe this festival, or the Rani Pokhari tank in central Kathmandu.

Chhath is a Hindu festival that is celebrated with great devotion and reverence in many parts of India. The festival is dedicated to the worship of the Sun God and his consort Usha, and it is believed that by performing the rituals of Chhath, one can attain blessings of health, prosperity, and well-being. From a philosophical perspective, Chhath can be seen as a celebration of the power of nature and the importance of gratitude and humility. By humbling ourselves before the Sun, we are reminded of our small place in the grand scheme of things and the need to be thankful for the blessings we receive. It also highlights the importance of balance between human and nature and how the nature's power could be used for human well-being.

Mani Rimdu

Nepali Calender : कार्तिक

Solar Calender : October - November

Active Region : Tengboche Temple, Thame Temple

This is a sacred ceremonies and series of events of empowerment. It is a sequence of nineteen days celebration, which concludes with three days public festival. The main first days of festival involves prayers, second day for colorful lama dancing, they wear brocade gown and wonderfully painted papier-mâché masks. Last day is for some humorous dances and chanting prayers. Hundred of local people and foreign tourist attend the performance.Lamas and Sherpa gather at the monastery for five days for the welfare of the world. Demons are quelled and the virtuous are rewarded. The monks wear elaborate mask, costumes’ and through a series ritualistic Lama dances, dramatize the triumph of Buddhism over Bon,

The festival is a profound expression of the interconnectedness of all things, as it brings together the spiritual and the secular, the traditional and the modern, the individual and the community. It serves as a reminder that true wisdom and enlightenment come not from solitary contemplation, but from the collective efforts of the community, and that true spiritual progress is rooted in the understanding of the interconnectedness of all things.

Lhosar

Nepali Calender : माघ

Solar Calender : January - February

Active Region : Hilly and Himalayan Region

Lhosar is widely celebrated by Nepalese ethnic groups who trace their heritage to Tibet – namely the Gurung, Tamang and Sherpa people. Lhosar is the first day of the new year, and each community celebrates the festival in their own way. Traditional dress is worn by young and old, and festivities are held across the country in cities and more remote regions.

Lhosar, also known as Losar, is a traditional festival celebrated by the Tibetan people. It marks the start of the Tibetan New Year and is a time of renewal and hope. On a philosophical level, Lhosar can be seen as a reminder of the cyclical nature of time and the importance of letting go of the past and embracing the future. It is a time to reflect on one's actions and to make amends for any mistakes made in the past year. The festival also serves as a reminder that all things are impermanent, and that we must strive to make the most of the present moment. Lhosar is a celebration of the human spirit and its ability to overcome challenges and start anew.

Basanta Panchami

Nepali Calender : माघ

Solar Calender : January - February

Active Region : Kathmandu , Nationwide

Devotees worship the Hindu goddess Saraswati – goddess of knowledge and creator of arts, education and music. Celebrated by both Hindus and Sikhs across Nepal and India, people often dress in yellow on this day, and kite-flying is also common. Basant Panchami also marks the end of winter and the beginning of spring.

Basanta Panchami is a Hindu festival that marks the arrival of spring, and it is celebrated with great fervor in Nepal. The festival is not only celebrated as a celebration of spring but also as a celebration of knowledge, wisdom, and culture. The festival is a reminder that just as spring brings new life and growth to the earth, knowledge and wisdom bring new life and growth to the mind and soul. The festival encourages individuals to seek out knowledge and wisdom, to embrace the beauty of the world around them and to appreciate the richness of their culture. It is also a reminder that the pursuit of knowledge is not only an individual pursuit but also a collective one, which brings people together and strengthens communities. It is a day to celebrate the beauty of nature and the power of knowledge and wisdom to transform our lives and the world around us.

Maha Shivaratri

Nepali Calender : फाल्गुण

Solar Calender : February - March

Active Region : Pashupatinath,Terai region,Nationwide

‘Shivaratri’ literally means the ‘night of Lord Shiva’, and on this day devout Hindus bathe early in the morning and then fast, before visiting Shiva temples for worship. The best place to witness the festival is at Pashupatinath temple in Kathmandu, where thousands of Sadhus (Hindu holy men) gather to smoke marijuana and hashish, considered dear to Lord Shiva. People also drink Bhang, a drink made by mixing ground nuts, spices, herbs and extracts of marijuana into milk.

Maha Shivaratri, is a Hindu festival that is celebrated annually in honor of Lord Shiva,the principal deities of Hinduism. From a philosophical perspective, the festival is believed to symbolize the victory of good over evil, and the triumph of spiritual knowledge over ignorance. It is a time for introspection and self-reflection, as well as a time to seek blessings and forgiveness from L ord Shiva. The festival is also believed to be an opportunity to seek union with the divine, and to achieve a state of spiritual enlightenment through devotion and contemplation. Ultimately, Maha Shivaratri is a celebration of the power of the human spirit to transcend the limitations of the physical world and attain true spiritual freedom.

Holi

Nepali Calender : फाल्गुण

Solar Calender : February - March

Active Region : Nationwide

Perhaps the best-known festival on this list, Holi is a crazily colourful and playful event held over 2 days. It is celebrated in the Terai on one day, and in Kathmandu and the hill regions on the next day. People smear coloured powders on their friends, relatives and family members, as well as taking part in giant water fights using coloured water and water balloons.

Holi, also known as the Festival of Colors, is a Hindu festival that symbolizes the triumph of good over evil, and the arrival of spring. It is celebrated by people of all ages, and is a time for forgiveness, coming together with loved ones, and spreading joy and love. On a philosophical level, Holi reminds us that just as the colors of spring bring new life and vitality to the world, we too have the ability to bring new life and vitality to our relationships, our communities, and ourselves. Through the act of forgiveness, we can let go of past hurt and grudges, and by coming together with loved ones, we can strengthen the bonds of love and friendship. The festival of Holi, therefore, is not just a celebration of the arrival of spring, but a celebration of the power of love and forgiveness to bring new life and vitality to our world.

Ghode Jatra

Nepali Calender : चैत्र

Solar Calender : MArch - April

Active Region : Kathmandu

Ghode Jatra is particularly important in the Kathmandu Valley and is celebrated to ward off the demonic Gurumapa. It is said the soul of the demon still lives underground at Tundikhel. To prevent him from rising again, a horse race is organised on this day by the Nepal Army.

Ghode Jatra, also known as the horse festival, is a celebration that has deep roots in the culture and philosophy of the people who observe it. This festival is a reminder that life is a continuous cycle of birth, growth, and death. The horse, a powerful and majestic animal, symbolizes the potential for greatness within us all. As the horse is paraded through the streets, we are reminded that we too must embrace our own power and potential, and strive to become the best version of ourselves. Ghode Jatra is not just a celebration of the horse, but of the human spirit and our ability to overcome obstacles and achieve great things. It is a reminder that we are all connected and that our actions, no matter how small, have the power to shape the world around us.

Chaitra Dashain

Nepali Calender : चैत्र

Solar Calender : March - April

Active Region : Nationwide

Chaite dashain is the last festival of the Hindu calendar and also known as the Small Dashain. It is also the major festival of the Hindus which carries great religious and cultural significance. The festival has two days of celebration. First day is Ram Nawami and the second Day is Dashami. Some parts of Nepal and India , Chaite Dashain is also known as the Chaitra navratri.

Chaitra Dashain is a festival that not only marks the end of a year and the beginning of a new one, but also serves as a reminder of the eternal cycle of life and death. It is a time to celebrate the triumph of good over evil and to reflect on the lessons learned from the past. The festival also encourages us to let go of the old, to embrace the new, and to look forward to the future with hope and optimism. It is a time to come together with family and friends, to share in the joys and struggles of life, and to be grateful for the blessings we have received. Ultimately, Chaitra Dashain is a celebration of the human spirit, which endures and thrives despite the constant changes and challenges of life.
